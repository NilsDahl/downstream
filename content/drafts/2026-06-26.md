## Chain 1: Middle East Supply Thaw Deflates Oil, Reshapes Rate Path

**The driver:** Qatar's force majeure expected to lapse in July, the Strait of Hormuz reopening to tanker traffic, and oil prices falling to pre-war levels — Brent at 72.7100 (-3.39%) and WTI at 69.1900 (-3.80%) — form a coherent supply-restoration shock that is repricing inflation expectations and sovereign yields simultaneously.

**The chain:**

**Supply shock reversal in energy:** Empty LNG tankers massing outside Qatar's export terminals, ADNOC vessels resuming AIS broadcasts, and market pricing of force majeure expiry in mid-July all point to a rapid normalization of Persian Gulf energy flows. Gasoline (RBOB) is the sharpest signal: down -6.97% to 2.8163, well outpacing crude itself, indicating the market is front-running downstream pass-through to consumer fuel prices. This is not a demand collapse — it is a supply restoration being priced with urgency.

**Inflation expectations collapse, dragging yields down:** With energy the marginal driver of headline CPI across the US, Europe, and Asia, a sudden 3-4% crude selloff directly compresses the near-term inflation trajectory. Going into today, the prior session had already seen US yields fall materially — the 5Y at 4.1700% (-10.0 bps), the 10Y at 4.4100% (-9.0 bps), and the 30Y at 4.8600% (-8.0 bps). The bull-steepener shaping up at the long end reflects markets reducing the inflation premium embedded since the conflict began, not pricing a growth scare (which would flatten or invert). The RBI's external MPC member flagging potential upward revision to growth above 7% as geopolitical risk fades corroborates this read: this is a supply-side relief rally in rates, not a recession signal.

**The Fed hawkishness tension:** Here is the explicit contradiction in the chain. The NPR report notes that under Chair Kevin Warsh, the Fed held rates despite inflation at a three-year high, and Fortune flags that bond markets are ignoring the hawkish signal embedded in $39 trillion of national debt. SOFR holds flat at 3.6200%, and the 1M bill actually ticked up +1.0 bps — the front end is not yet pricing cuts. The long end rallying -8 to -10 bps while the front end is anchored compresses the 2s10s spread in a way that reflects the market betting oil-driven disinflation will do the Fed's work for it, even if Warsh refuses to blink. If energy retraces, this bet unwinds violently.

**Dollar softens, but incompletely:** A falling inflation premium typically weakens the dollar by reducing the rate differential that has sustained DXY. Today, DXY falls to 101.2050 (-0.22%), EUR/USD rises to 1.1405 (+0.45%), and USD/CHF falls to 0.8085 (-0.50%). But USD/JPY at 161.6130 (-0.09%) barely moves despite the BOJ hiking to its highest rate since 1995 and Governor Ueda reiterating upside inflation risk — the yen remains structurally weak because the BOJ's pace is still far below the Fed's held level. The commodity currencies tell a split story: USD/CAD falls -0.36% (Canada benefits from oil stabilizing), but AUD/USD gains only +0.03%, reflecting copper's conflicted picture (up +1.08% to 6.1360 on peace-talk optimism, but the prior session saw it hold losses on dollar and Fed pressure). The dollar weakness is real but uneven, shaped by which economies are net energy importers versus exporters.

**Equity divergence — the rotation signal:** US equities are essentially flat at the index level: S&P 500 -0.01% at 7357.4902, Nasdaq -0.46% at 25358.5996, but Russell 2000 +0.71% at 3007.8601. This is a textbook rate-relief rotation — small caps are more sensitive to domestic borrowing costs, so falling long yields lift them disproportionately. The Nasdaq underperforms because tech's valuation is duration-sensitive in the opposite direction: lower yields should help, but the VIX jumping +7.25% to 20.2600 and the MarketWatch tech fear gauge near a two-decade high suggest that volatility repricing is weighing on high-multiple names specifically. European equities sold off sharply — DAX -1.18% to 24700.5508, Euro Stoxx 50 -0.79% — likely because lower oil benefits European consumers but the selloff also compresses energy-sector earnings, and the ECB's Lagarde seeing no need for a more forceful policy response removes any stimulus catalyst. Asia was harder hit: Nikkei -4.15% to 69360.8828, Hang Seng -1.76%, CSI 300 -1.48%, the Nikkei's drop amplified by the BOJ rate hike at 1.00% (the highest since 1995) compressing Japanese equity valuations even as the BOJ's Ueda flags AI-export demand as a cushion.

**Precious metals hold, industrial metals split:** Gold at 4067.0000 (+0.91%) and silver at 58.7700 (+0.72%) recovering modestly after falling below $4,000 is the tension node. Capital Economics sees further downside over 18 months, and silver has shed over 20% year-to-date per Reuters. But today's bid in gold alongside falling real yields (nominal yields down, oil-driven inflation expectations falling but uncertainty remaining) suggests a safe-haven component persisting — the VIX spike at 20.2600 is providing a floor. Copper's +1.08% gain on peace-talk progress contrasts with nickel's -5.80% and iron ore's -4.76%, the latter two tracking the China demand-uncertainty story: the PBOC adviser flagging a potential rate cut suggests Chinese policymakers are not confident domestic demand is strong enough to absorb the commodity complex without additional easing.

**What to watch:** First, watch whether the US 2Y yield (currently 4.1100%) breaks below 4.00% — that level would signal markets have moved from pricing disinflation to pricing cuts, forcing a confrontation with the Warsh Fed's hold posture and triggering a sharper dollar decline. Second, track Brent at the 70.00 level: a break below it would accelerate the inflation-expectations compression and validate the long-end rally, but a rebound toward 75.00 would expose the duration trade as premature and risk a sharp reversal in the Russell 2000 versus Nasdaq spread. Third, watch the BOJ's next policy signal following Ueda's hospital discharge and return Tuesday — if USD/JPY fails to break below 160.00 despite the rate hike already delivered, it confirms that yen weakness is structural and not policy-correctable at the current pace, with implications for Japanese import-cost inflation that cuts against the BOJ's own disinflation narrative.
