**The driver:** Kevin Warsh's hawkish debut as Fed chair — holding rates steady while signaling future hikes and projecting the fed funds rate at 3.8% by end-2026 — triggered a sharp bear-flattening of the U.S. yield curve, with the 2Y rising +15.0 bps to 4.2000% while the 30Y held flat at 4.9300%.

**The chain:**

**Warsh reprices the short end:** The Fed's median dot now places the terminal rate a quarter-point above the current range, and Warsh's press conference made clear this is not a pause-friendly chair. Traders rapidly priced hike risk into front-end Treasuries: the 1Y surged +14.0 bps to 3.9800%, the 6M jumped +10.0 bps to 3.9100%, and the 2Y climbed +15.0 bps to 4.2000%. The long end was unmoved — 30Y flat at 4.9300% — meaning the curve bear-flattened violently, a textbook signal that markets believe tighter policy will succeed in crushing demand before it becomes a structural inflation problem.

**Equities reprice the discount rate:** A hawkish Fed removes the "policy put" that equity markets had been trading on. Higher short rates raise the discount rate applied to future earnings, compressing multiples, and Warsh's debut produced the worst "Fed day" S&P 500 performance for a new chair since 1994. The mechanism is straightforward: if the 2Y clears 4.2000% and the market prices another hike, equity risk premiums must widen to compensate — particularly painful for long-duration growth names that had benefited from the prior easy-money assumption.

**Gold caught in a crossfire:** Gold fell as the hawkish Fed raised the opportunity cost of holding a zero-yield asset — higher real short rates directly compress gold's appeal. Simultaneously, the U.S.-Iran interim peace deal removed a geopolitical risk premium that had been supporting the metal. The two forces reinforced each other on the downside, confirming gold's decline is not idiosyncratic noise but a clean expression of the day's two dominant themes: policy tightening and geopolitical de-escalation.

**Oil collapses on dual supply shock:** WTI dropped -4.48% to 84.6500 and Brent fell -4.83% to 84.3600 — one of the day's largest moves in absolute risk terms. The mechanism is supply, not demand: the U.S.-Iran deal, combined with Iraq formally preparing to boost exports when Hormuz reopens and Qatar returning an empty LNG tanker through the Strait for the first time since the war, signals an imminent reversal of the Middle East supply disruption. The IEA simultaneously flagged a supply glut next year. This is a structural repricing of the energy risk premium, not a short squeeze unwind.

**Oil's collapse creates a disinflationary tension with Warsh's hawkishness:** Here the day presents a genuine contradiction. Warsh cited energy costs and geopolitical tensions as justification for signaling hikes — but oil just fell nearly 5%. If crude remains at these levels or continues lower, the inflation impulse driving the hawkish pivot weakens materially. The €STR jumped +25.1 bps and the ECB already hiked citing energy-driven second-round effects, but a sustained oil decline would erode that rationale across all G10 central banks simultaneously. The BOE held, with Bailey explicitly noting energy remains above pre-war levels — but a Hormuz reopening changes that calculus fast.

**BOJ hike tightens the global liquidity envelope:** The Bank of Japan raised rates to their highest since 1995, adding a third major central bank tightening in the same 24-hour window as the Fed's hawkish signal and the ECB's recent hike. The mechanism: BOJ normalization reduces the yen carry trade's attractiveness, pulling capital back toward JGBs and compressing the global pool of cheap funding that has supported risk assets. This acts as a structural amplifier of the Warsh shock — it is not just the Fed removing accommodation, but a synchronized global tightening at a moment when equity multiples and credit spreads were priced for the opposite.
