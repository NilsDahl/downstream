**The driver:** Kevin Warsh's inaugural FOMC meeting held rates steady but delivered a hawkish-leaning press conference — Warsh described an internal "good family fight" on policy — leaving the Fed's door explicitly open to a hike, which pushed the S&P 500 down 1.2% and reshuffled the yield curve.

**The chain:**

**Warsh signals asymmetric risk, curve bull-flattens:** The Fed held rates steady, but Warsh's language flagged upside inflation risk and FOMC division over whether the next move is a hike. The market's initial read was a steepener threat, but the actual curve outcome is a bull-flattening: the 10Y fell 4.0 bps to 4.4300% and the 30Y fell 4.0 bps to 4.9300%, while the 2Y only fell 2.0 bps to 4.0500%. This pattern is consistent with the front end pricing a "hold for longer" rather than an imminent hike — the long end catching a mild bid as growth expectations soften on the equity selloff.

**Equity repricing on the policy uncertainty premium:** A Fed that describes its own deliberations as a "family fight" and holds the hike option live introduces genuine policy path uncertainty — the most damaging input for equity multiples, which discount future cash flows. The S&P 500's 1.2% decline reflects that uncertainty premium being added back, not a specific recession signal. SOFR dropped 6.0 bps to 3.6300%, suggesting money markets trimmed near-term tightening expectations even as Warsh's rhetoric pointed the other direction — a tension between market positioning and Fed communication that has not resolved.

**SOFR-to-front-end divergence signals credibility test:** The 6-month and 1-year Treasury yields are unchanged at 3.8100% and 3.8400% respectively, while SOFR fell sharply to 3.6300%. This divergence suggests that overnight funding markets moved on liquidity dynamics (possibly PBOC's new rate regime amplifying global dollar funding ease) rather than on a clear re-read of Fed policy. The PBOC's refinement of its toolkit, flagged as dampening money-market volatility, may be importing a modest easing signal into short-end dollar rates through cross-currency channels.

**Geopolitical relief partially offsets the hawkish macro tone:** The interim US-Iran peace deal sent an LNG tanker back through the Strait of Hormuz for the first time since the Iran war began — a concrete signal that energy supply chains are reopening. The IEA simultaneously slashed its global oil demand forecast, framing the earlier supply shock as having already triggered demand destruction. These two forces — supply returning while demand has been structurally impaired — are structurally bearish for energy prices, which would, if sustained, actually ease the inflation path Warsh cited as a risk and create a contradiction with the hawkish Fed posture.

**Eurozone and BOJ add synchronized tightening pressure:** The ECB hiked for the first time since 2023 — the €STR jumped 25.1 bps to 2.1820% — citing Iran-war energy costs feeding second-round inflation effects, with Lagarde warning the 2% target won't be reached until H2 2027. The BOJ hiked to its highest rate since 1995. Three major central banks tightening within the same 24-hour window establishes a global policy tightening pulse that reinforces the message from Warsh: the easing cycle is not just paused, it may be reversing. EZ longer yields barely moved (10Y -0.7 bps to 2.9855%), suggesting markets believe the ECB hike is close to terminal — a bet that will be tested if energy prices stay elevated.

**What to watch:** Monitor the US 2Y yield at 4.0500% — a move above 4.15% would signal that markets are beginning to price a Warsh hike, collapsing the current divergence between SOFR and the front end. Track LNG tanker transit frequency through Hormuz: sustained passage would pressure energy prices lower, directly undercutting the ECB's and Fed's inflation-driven hawkishness and forcing a reassessment of the global tightening pulse. Watch whether the €STR's 25.1 bps single-session jump stabilizes or reverses — an outsized overnight rate move of that magnitude in a mature market typically reflects a technical fixing or policy implementation change, and its persistence (or correction) will clarify whether the ECB hike is fully priced into European short rates.
