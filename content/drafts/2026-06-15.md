**The driver:** The US-Iran peace deal and Strait of Hormuz reopening sent Brent crude down -4.79% to $83.1500 and WTI down -5.38% to $80.3100, triggering a synchronized global risk-on repricing across equities, bonds, FX, and metals.

**The chain:**

**Oil shock reversal compresses inflation expectations → sovereign yields rally hard:** The Hormuz reopening removes the core supply-side inflation driver that had forced the ECB to hike for the first time since 2023 and kept the Fed on hold. US Treasuries repriced aggressively across the curve — the 10Y fell -10.0 bps to 4.4500%, the 5Y fell -9.0 bps to 4.1800%, and the 2Y fell -8.0 bps to 4.0500% — as traders unwound war-premium inflation risk premia embedded in nominal yields. The eurozone front end moved even more sharply, with the EZ 6M falling -7.5 bps to 2.3499% and EZ 1Y falling -6.6 bps to 2.4794%, directly pricing out the rate-hike path Lagarde had just begun. The yield rally is steeper at the front end in both jurisdictions, which is the mechanically correct response to a supply-side inflation shock being unwound.

**Risk-on ignites global equities, with the largest gains in most oil-war-exposed markets:** The Nikkei 225 surged +4.99% to 69317.5000 — Japan imports nearly all its crude and had been paying a severe war tax on energy costs. The DAX gained +1.44% to 24989.8203 and the CAC 40 +1.22% to 8452.4102, reflecting European manufacturing's energy cost sensitivity. US indices participated but more modestly — S&P 500 +0.50% to 7431.4600, Nasdaq 100 +0.64% to 29635.9492 — consistent with a relief rally rather than a fresh growth catalyst, since the US is a net oil producer and the direct benefit is smaller. VIX collapsed -7.07% to 16.4300, confirming the move is broad-based risk repricing, not a single sector rotation.

**Dollar softens as geopolitical risk premium drains:** The DXY fell -0.26% to 99.4930 as the safe-haven bid for dollars partially unwound. EM currencies outperformed: USD/BRL fell -1.45% to 5.0396, USD/ZAR fell -0.64% to 16.1755 — the rand move is doubly explained by the South Africa rate-hike bets being cut as oil-driven inflation fears fade. EUR/USD rose +0.38% to 1.1620 despite the ECB now facing a dilemma: Lagarde had just hiked citing energy pass-through, but the oil shock that justified the hike is now reversing. EUR strength here reflects dollar weakness and broad risk appetite, not a genuine rerating of ECB hawkishness.

**Precious metals surge — the anomaly that requires explanation:** Gold rose +3.56% to 4365.2002, Silver +4.59% to 70.9750, Platinum +5.46% to 1802.6000, and Palladium +6.64% to 1361.0000. This is directionally inconsistent with a simple risk-on/lower-inflation story — falling inflation expectations and a weaker safe-haven impulse should cap gold. The most coherent mechanism is dollar depreciation amplifying metals prices, combined with industrial demand re-rating (Platinum and Palladium are autocatalyst metals, and a growth-positive peace deal lifts auto demand expectations). The gold move also reflects lingering credibility concerns: with FOMC Chair Warsh signaling a hawkish stance and the ECB caught mid-hike-cycle by a reversing shock, policy uncertainty remains elevated, sustaining real-asset demand.

**BOJ hike and FOMC inaugural meeting under Warsh create a second-order rate divergence signal:** The BOJ is expected to hike to the highest level since 1995, yet USD/JPY is near-flat at 160.1250 (-0.00%). The yen is not strengthening despite risk-on and an imminent BOJ hike — this is a tension. The absence of Governor Ueda introduces communication uncertainty that may be suppressing yen appreciation; markets may be discounting the press conference quality. Meanwhile, the Warsh Fed is signaled as hawkish into an FOMC meeting on June 17, even as oil — the primary inflation driver — just dropped sharply. If Warsh acknowledges the oil reversal, the market's hawkish repricing of the Fed could rapidly unwind, sending front-end yields lower still.
