**The driver:** The ECB's first interest rate hike since 2023 — triggered by Iran-war energy price pressures — is the day's primary catalyst, setting a divergent policy signal against a US curve that rallied modestly and UK/BOJ uncertainty that kept risk sentiment cautious but not broken.

**The chain:**

**ECB hikes into an energy shock → European yields reprice higher:** The ECB's rate increase pushed the entire eurozone curve upward, with EZ 5Y leading at +2.0 bps to 2.7589% and EZ 10Y rising +1.7 bps to 3.1163%. The mechanism is straightforward: a central bank tightening in response to cost-push inflation from Hormuz disruption forces front-end repricing and drags the belly and long end higher as the market prices a sustained hiking path. The €STR held flat at 1.9300%, but the term structure shift confirms markets believe this hike is not isolated. Lagarde's explicit warning that inflation returns to target only by the second half of 2027 tells the market the ECB is not nearly done.

**Divergence from the US curve → EUR/USD under modest pressure:** While eurozone yields rose, the US curve bull-flattened — the 10Y fell -3.0 bps to 4.5300% and the 30Y fell -2.0 bps to 5.0100%, while the 1Y rose +5.0 bps to 3.9000%, steepening the short end. This divergence — ECB hiking while US rates eased — would ordinarily support the euro, but EUR/USD fell -0.13% to 1.1521 and DXY rose +0.27% to 100.2160. The tension here is real: the euro is weakening despite a relatively hawkish ECB signal, suggesting the market is pricing the energy shock as a net growth drag on Europe more than a hawkish catalyst, with Lagarde's own growth warning reinforcing that read.

**Energy prices anchored near the ceiling → stagflation pricing in Europe:** Brent Crude sits at 92.8200 (-0.30%) and WTI at 90.1600 (+0.14%) — both near $90-$93 with the Hormuz strait disrupted and Reuters confirming Israel-Lebanon strikes added $2.57 to US crude. The ECB is hiking into a supply-driven inflation shock, not a demand-driven one, which is textbook stagflation risk. Natural Gas fell -2.98% to 3.0900, possibly on the Malaysia-Japan LNG deal news, providing a modest offset, but petroleum products remain elevated. The ECB cannot cure a supply shock with rate hikes; it can only suppress demand further, which is exactly the growth drag Lagarde acknowledged.

**BOJ governor absence → yen stays weak, Japanese equities flat:** USD/JPY rose +0.08% to 160.5180 and Nikkei 225 added only +0.06% to 64217.2695 — a muted session consistent with genuine policy uncertainty. Markets still price a BOJ hike next week, per the headlines, but the absence of Ueda from the press conference creates a communication vacuum. A hawkish hike without credible forward guidance from the governor risks either an overcorrection in yen or a dismissal of the signal. EUR/JPY fell -0.07% to 184.8710, reflecting competing pressures from ECB hawkishness and BOJ uncertainty rather than a clean directional trade.

**Risk assets hold but gold miners diverge from bullion:** US equities rose — S&P 500 +0.32% to 7290.3701, Nasdaq 100 +0.83% to 28745.8262, Russell 2000 +1.50% to 2878.0217 — and VIX fell -4.46% to 21.2300, suggesting the ECB hike was well-telegraphed and not a shock. Gold fell -0.15% to 4102.0000, consistent with the Barron's report of gold miners declining sharply (Newmont -4.2%, Barrick -3.5%, Agnico Eagle -4.4%). The divergence between gold holding near $4102 and miners selling off sharply signals cost-of-capital pressure: higher European rates and a stronger dollar compress mining equity multiples faster than they reduce the spot commodity itself, especially when energy costs (the largest mining input) remain elevated.
