**The driver:** US equities collapsed across the board — the S&P 500 fell -1.62%, the Nasdaq 100 -1.98%, and the Dow Jones -1.87% — while European indices simultaneously rallied over 1%, a sharp transatlantic divergence that is the session's most anomalous and explanatory signal.

**The chain:**

**Transatlantic rotation, not global risk-off:** If this were a genuine global risk-off episode, European equities would have sold off alongside US equities. Instead, the Euro Stoxx 50 gained +1.06%, the CAC 40 +0.95%, and the FTSE 100 +0.92% — all while the S&P 500 lost -1.62%. This pattern points to active capital rotation out of US risk assets and into European ones, likely driven by relative valuation, dollar fatigue, or a US-specific negative catalyst (earnings shock, policy surprise, or geopolitical news). The divergence is too clean to be coincidental.

**US rates confirm domestic stress, not global duration selling:** In a pure risk-off flight-to-quality, US Treasury yields would fall across the curve — and they did, but selectively. The 10Y fell -3.0 bps to 4.5300% and the 5Y fell -3.0 bps to 4.2600%, consistent with a bid for safety. The critical anomaly is the 1Y yield, which *rose* +5.0 bps to 3.9000%, steepening the front end of the curve in isolation. This kink suggests a near-term repricing event — possibly a Fed communication or a data release — is being priced into the 1Y node specifically, even as the rest of the curve rallies.

**European yields move in the opposite direction — tightening, not easing:** While US yields fell, Eurozone yields rose uniformly across the curve: EZ 2Y +1.6 bps to 2.6354%, EZ 5Y +2.0 bps to 2.7589%, EZ 10Y +1.7 bps to 3.1163%. Rising yields alongside rising equity prices in Europe signals that capital inflows into European risk assets are strong enough to simultaneously push equity prices higher and sell European bonds. This is a classic "hot money inflow" signature — investors buying European equities at the margin, not European duration.

**FX corroborates rotation but flags a limit:** EUR/USD is only +0.05% at 1.1541, and the DXY is actually +0.11% at 100.0610 despite the US equity selloff. This is the session's internal tension: if capital is leaving the US, the dollar should weaken more aggressively. Instead, the dollar is marginally firmer, suggesting the rotation is equity-to-equity (US stocks sold, European stocks bought) rather than a full currency-level flight from dollar assets. USD/JPY at +0.09% to 160.5210 confirms the yen is not receiving a traditional safe-haven bid, which would be expected in a broad risk-off move.

**Commodity currencies bear the US equity contagion:** AUD/USD fell -0.36% to 0.6998 and NZD/USD fell -0.37% to 0.5786 — both commodity-linked, risk-sensitive currencies that typically track global growth expectations. Their weakness contradicts the European equity strength and aligns with the US equity decline, suggesting global growth optimism is not behind the European rally. USD/SEK rose +0.49% to 9.5293 and EUR/SEK +0.51% to 10.9946, an unusually sharp SEK underperformance that stands out even within the rotation narrative and warrants monitoring as a potential idiosyncratic Swedish macro signal.

**VIX collapse creates a contradiction worth naming:** The VIX fell -6.17% to 20.8500 on a session where the S&P 500 dropped -1.62%. A falling VIX during an equity selloff is unusual; it suggests the market is not buying protection against further downside, implying the sell-off may be viewed as orderly or rotation-driven rather than panic-driven. If this is a structured institutional rotation rather than a fear-driven liquidation, the VIX reading is internally consistent with the European rally — but it also means downside hedges are cheap if the US-specific catalyst proves more durable than one session.
