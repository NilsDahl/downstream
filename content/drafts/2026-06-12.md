**The chain:**

**Geopolitical risk premium collapses in oil:** A US-Iran ceasefire framework directly threatens the war-premium embedded in crude since the Strait of Hormuz closure in late February. Brent fell -3.37% to $87.33 and WTI -3.23% to $84.88, with heating oil dropping -3.09% and RBOB gasoline -1.66% — energy complex moves that are broad and consistent, not noise. The critical caveat is Tehran's pushback and the Cushing inventory floor sitting less than 2 mmbbls above operational minimum: the market is pricing hope, not delivery, and the supply structure beneath the deal remains fragile.

**Lower energy costs re-price global inflation expectations lower:** Energy is the single largest near-term driver of headline CPI across G10 economies. Crude falling sharply mechanically reduces the forward path of inflation, which directly eases pressure on central banks currently hiking into an energy shock. This is the mechanism behind the EZ yield curve bull-flattening: the EZ 2Y fell -2.6 bps, EZ 5Y -3.9 bps, and EZ 10Y -3.6 bps — duration rallying hardest at the belly and long end, consistent with markets trimming terminal rate expectations. The ECB hiked for the first time since 2023 this week on Iran-driven energy costs; a durable deal would arrest that tightening cycle mid-stride.

**Tension: UK gilts do not confirm the eurozone rally:** While EZ sovereign yields fell across the curve, UK gilts moved in the opposite direction — Gilt 5Y +1.7 bps, Gilt 10Y +1.4 bps, Gilt 20Y +1.1 bps. This divergence signals UK-specific inflation or fiscal concerns that are not being resolved by an Iran deal, likely reflecting UK domestic wage and services inflation dynamics that are structurally stickier than eurozone energy pass-through. The bond market is not reading this as a clean global disinflation signal.

**Risk-on equity rally, led by Europe and Asia with deepest war exposure:** Euro Stoxx 50 surged +2.16%, DAX +1.76%, CAC +1.83%, and FTSE +1.63% — all outperforming US indices — because European growth had been most directly impaired by energy import costs from the Iran shock. Asian indices followed the same logic: Nikkei +2.81%, Hang Seng +1.93%, ASX +1.98%. The VIX collapsed -9.05% to 17.68, confirming this is a broad derisking of the war-premium tail, not a sector rotation. US equities participated more modestly: S&P 500 +0.50%, Nasdaq 100 +0.64%, consistent with US markets having priced less downside from the conflict to begin with.

**Gold's +3.05% surge contradicts the simple risk-on narrative:** A classic risk-on move should pressure gold as safe-haven demand unwinds. Instead, gold surged +3.05% to $4,215.00, silver +6.22% to $67.859, platinum +2.80%, and palladium +3.37% — the entire precious metals complex rallying hard. Industrial metals confirmed genuine demand optimism: copper +2.74% to $6.4305, zinc +2.77%. The gold move is best read not as safe-haven buying but as a real-rate and dollar-debasement trade: if the Iran deal accelerates a Fed pivot away from the hawkish Warsh trajectory, lower real yields are bullish for gold independent of risk appetite. It also aligns with the CNBC report noting gold was at a 6-month low earlier in the week — this is a sharp reversal of that trend, potentially technically significant.

**FOMC June 17 now a live re-pricing event:** New Fed Chair Warsh enters his inaugural meeting facing a rapidly shifting backdrop: oil falling sharply reduces the energy-inflation argument for additional hikes, but US CPI reached a three-year high of 4.2% and services inflation remains structural. Markets had been pricing the possibility of a rate hike in Warsh's dot plot. A durable Iran deal weakens the energy-inflation leg of that argument, creating a binary: if Warsh signals hikes are off the table, front-end rates rally and the dollar weakens further from DXY's current -0.11% at 99.75; if he maintains the hawkish tilt citing non-energy inflation, the curve steepens and risk assets give back some of today's gains.
