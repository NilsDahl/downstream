**The driver:** The US-Iran peace deal and Hormuz reopening agreement has collapsed the geopolitical risk premium embedded in energy markets and sovereign yields, with Eurozone rates falling sharply (EZ 10Y -6.0 bps to 3.0089%) while US yields paradoxically rose (US 10Y +3.0 bps to 4.4800%), revealing a split between relief-driven disinflation in Europe and a hawkish Fed reset under Warsh.

**The chain:**

**Hormuz reopens → energy risk premium unwinds:** The signed US-Iran agreement, with Trump's explicit commitment to Hormuz reopening, triggered a collapse in the war-driven oil risk premium that had been embedded in global assets for months. Qatar's plan to restore LNG export capacity within two weeks of reopening amplifies the forward supply signal, compressing energy forward curves. Commodity analysts cited in CNBC note the oil shock is "far from over," but the market's immediate repricing reflects relief, not resolution.

**Oil supply shock reversal → Eurozone inflation path repriced lower:** The ECB had hiked rates for the first time since 2023 explicitly because Iran-war energy costs were driving inflation — Lagarde had warned of second-round effects feeding through the economy. With oil prices tumbling on the deal, that tightening rationale evaporates, and traders are now pricing the ECB path as materially less aggressive. The evidence is immediate and clean: the entire Eurozone curve fell sharply, with EZ 2Y down -5.9 bps to 2.5185%, EZ 5Y down -7.3 bps to 2.6445%, and EZ 10Y down -6.0 bps to 3.0089% — front-end leading, which is the classic signal of a central bank rate path being repriced lower.

**US yields rise despite the deal → Warsh Fed hawkishness dominates domestic pricing:** Here lies the central tension. Where European yields fell on the Iran relief trade, US yields rose across the board: US 2Y +4.0 bps to 4.0900%, US 5Y +3.0 bps to 4.2100%, US 10Y +3.0 bps to 4.4800%. With Kevin Warsh chairing his inaugural FOMC meeting on June 17, market expectations are coalescing around a Fed that acknowledges rate-hike optionality — not cuts. The SOFR fixing itself moved +5.0 bps to 3.6500%, suggesting near-term funding conditions are tightening at the margin. The Iran deal removes one inflation tailwind (energy), but Warsh's hawkish signaling and sticky non-energy inflation mean the Fed's reaction function is diverging sharply from the ECB's.

**US-Europe yield divergence → DXY and EM currency dynamics bifurcate:** Rising US yields against falling European yields widens the rate differential in the dollar's favor, mechanically supporting the DXY. Yet EM currencies — led by the South African rand — are rallying, per Bloomberg, as lower oil reduces EM current account stress and South African rate-hike bets are being scaled back. This creates a bifurcated EM picture: oil-importing EMs benefit from cheaper energy and lower imported inflation; oil-exporting EM sovereigns face the inverse. The rand leading EM FX gains is consistent with South Africa's oil import dependency and its rate path sensitivity.

**BOJ hike → JPY carry unwind risk compounds global duration volatility:** The Bank of Japan hiked to its highest rate since 1995, with the yen still near historic lows. This is not a relief trade — it is structural tightening that increases the cost of JPY-funded carry trades that have been long global risk assets and duration. A stronger yen impulse from further BOJ hikes would force carry unwind, pressuring both equities and bonds globally. The Gilt curve's decline (Gilt 10Y -5.5 bps to 4.8997%, Gilt 5Y -8.0 bps to 4.3392%) partially reflects Iran relief, but BOE Governor Bailey's ongoing defense of QT and warnings about the UK's economic trajectory suggest UK duration is not a clean safe haven.
