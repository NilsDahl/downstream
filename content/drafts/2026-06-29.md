## Chain 1: Middle East Ceasefire Fragility Splits Oil From Risk Assets

**The driver:** Weekend tit-for-tat US-Iran strikes near the Strait of Hormuz — including a supertanker hit — undermined ceasefire optimism, pushing Brent crude up 1.68% to $73.2000 even as equities broadly held gains on a subsequent "halt attacks" agreement.

**The chain:**

**The Hormuz tension premium reasserts itself:** The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical oil chokepoint — roughly a fifth of global petroleum flows through it. A struck tanker and renewed exchanges between US and Iranian forces over the weekend forced markets to re-price the risk that last week's ceasefire was premature. Brent crude rose to $73.2000 (+1.68%) and WTI to $70.0000 (+1.11%), partially clawing back the war-premium that had been unwinding. Pakistan's emergency LNG purchases this week — scrambling for supply after Hormuz disruptions choked flows — illustrate that this isn't merely a financial-market abstraction; physical supply chains are already impaired.

**The odd one out — distillates diverge from crude:** Here the data presents a genuine tension. While crude rose sharply, Gasoline (RBOB) fell -3.40% to $2.8566 and Heating Oil dropped -1.79% to $3.1508. Refined products moving opposite to their feedstock suggests the market is reading the Hormuz flare-up as a *crude supply disruption* rather than a demand-side energy shock — higher upstream prices, but muted pass-through expectations, possibly because the subsequent "halt attacks" agreement capped fears of a prolonged refinery/shipping shutdown. This divergence is worth watching: if crude stays elevated and distillates don't recover, margins for refiners compress.

**The Fed-inflation knot tightens:** Going into today, the prior session saw US yields mostly tick lower — the 2Y at 4.0900% (-2.0 bps), the 10Y at 4.4000% (-1.0 bps) — despite a hawkish backdrop. Minneapolis Fed President Kashkari explicitly shifted from expecting one cut to expecting one hike this year, citing energy-driven inflation (headline PCE already at 4.1%, the highest since April 2023). Fed Chair Warsh has signaled the same direction. Higher oil means goods cost more to produce and transport, keeping inflation elevated and giving the Fed political cover to hike — precisely the scenario that had previously knocked equities. The prior-session yield curve is not yet pricing a dramatic tightening, but SOFR at 3.6400% (+2.0 bps) at the very front end reflects incremental hawkish drift.

**Gold's strange silence amid geopolitical noise:** Gold is effectively flat at $4078.5000 (-0.00%), which is notable. Normally a safe-haven surge in geopolitical risk would lift gold — but the Bloomberg headline captures the paradox directly: gold fell *because* US-Iran tension fanned inflation concerns, which strengthened the case for Fed rate hikes, which lift the real (inflation-adjusted) return on holding dollars rather than gold. A stronger-dollar, higher-rate environment is gold's enemy even when geopolitics are its friend. The DXY at 101.2540 (-0.10%) is only modestly softer today, offering gold limited relief. Gold is now on its fourth straight week of slides per Investing.com — the Fed hike narrative is winning over the haven narrative for now.

**Equities absorb the shock via divergent geography:** US indices are essentially flat — S&P 500 at 7354.0200 (-0.05%), Nasdaq at 25297.6191 (-0.24%) — consistent with the "ceasefire pause restored" reading by mid-session. The more interesting signal is in Asia: Hang Seng surged +1.56% to 23026.6797 and CSI 300 gained +1.21% to 4926.9209, driven partly by the Baidu AI chip IPO story ($50 billion HK listing) and Samsung/SK Hynix investment pledges supporting the regional AI investment thesis. China tech is catching a separate bid — the record inflow into China Tech ETFs even during a global chip selloff underscores that Chinese domestic investors are decoupling their outlook from global semiconductor sentiment. European equities (DAX +0.23%, Euro Stoxx 50 +0.16%) held steady despite Spanish inflation surprising to the upside, with the ECB's Lagarde explicitly saying no "more forceful" response is needed — a dovish signal that kept EZ yields well-contained, with the EZ 10Y at 2.9160% (-2.2 bps) in the prior session.

**The EUR's quiet strength and what it signals:** EUR/USD at 1.1405 (+0.38%) is rising even as oil — a major eurozone import cost — increases, which is counterintuitive at first glance. The mechanism: Lagarde's dovish hold signal relative to Warsh's hawkish lean typically weakens the euro, but the ECB's relative calm is actually reassuring sovereign credit markets. Eurozone lending to firms hit a three-year high in May, suggesting the credit channel remains open and the real economy is not seizing. That underlying credit growth, combined with receding tail-risk from the Middle East (temporary ceasefire), is enough to push EUR/USD modestly higher on the day. The NOK (USD/NOK +0.63%) is notably weakening against this trend — as a petrocurrency (a currency whose value correlates with oil export revenues), Norway's krone is being pulled in two directions: higher oil is positive, but Hormuz disruption risk clouds the demand outlook.

**What to watch:**
1. **Brent crude and the $75 level**: If tit-for-tat strikes resume before peace talks conclude this week, Brent reclaiming $75 would likely force a reassessment of Fed hike pricing in SOFR futures — watch whether the US 2Y yield (currently 4.0900%) breaks back above 4.15%, which would be the clearest sign bond markets are pricing in Kashkari's threatened hike.
2. **RBOB Gasoline vs. crude spread**: Today's -3.40% RBOB move against +1.68% crude is an anomaly. If gasoline recovers toward crude's level in coming sessions, it confirms the inflation pass-through is real and broadening — a direct test of whether the Fed's inflation concern is supply-chain-deep or merely headline-crude-shallow.
3. **Gold's $4,000 floor**: Bloomberg noted gold fell to *near* $4,000 before recovering. If the US-Iran halt-attacks agreement holds into the week and SOFR continues its upward drift, watch whether spot gold breaks below $4,000 — that would confirm the rate-hike narrative has fully eclipsed the safe-haven bid, with significant implications for long-duration assets broadly.
