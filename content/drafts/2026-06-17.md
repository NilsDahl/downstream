**The driver:** Kevin Warsh's inaugural FOMC meeting delivered a rate hold but an explicit hawkish tilt — officials were split on whether to hike, Warsh described a "good family fight" on policy, and the Fed flagged elevated inflation through year-end — sending the S&P 500 down 1.2% and repricing the front-end of the U.S. curve.

---

**The chain:**

**Fed hold with a hawkish conditional:** The FOMC held rates steady but the internal split over a potential hike, combined with Warsh's combative press conference framing, signaled that the next move is more likely up than down. Markets read this as a policy regime shift: Warsh is not Powell, and the Fed's reaction function has tightened. The front-end dropped modestly — US 2Y fell 2.0 bps to 4.0500% — but this reflects relief the hike didn't come today, not a dovish re-rating of the path ahead.

**Equity repricing on a higher-for-longer Fed:** The S&P 500's 1.2% decline is the direct mechanical consequence: if the terminal rate is now skewed higher and the cut cycle is effectively cancelled, the discount rate embedded in equity valuations must rise. Growth and duration-sensitive sectors bear the most weight. This is not a growth-scare selloff — it is a cost-of-capital repricing triggered by a policy signal, which is why the long end also fell (US 10Y -4.0 bps to 4.4300%, US 30Y -4.0 bps to 4.9300%), suggesting the market believes tightening now raises the probability of demand destruction later.

**Bull steepener forming at the long end:** The simultaneous front-end hold and long-end rally (10Y and 30Y both -4.0 bps) is the critical internal tension in the curve. A hawkish Fed that kills the cycle should flatten or invert — but instead the long end is rallying. The mechanism: markets are pricing that Warsh-era tightening compresses future nominal growth, pulling long-run rate expectations down even as the near-term path steepens. The US 10Y at 4.4300% vs. the 2Y at 4.0500% keeps the curve positively sloped — watch whether that spread widens further as a signal of recession pricing.

**ECB hikes independently, divergence widens:** The ECB raised rates for the first time since 2023 — Lagarde citing Iran-war energy passthrough and second-round effects — with the €STR jumping 25.1 bps to 2.1820%. This is a separate monetary tightening vector: European inflation is being driven by an energy supply shock, not demand, meaning the ECB is tightening into a growth headwind. EZ 10Y fell 0.7 bps to 2.9855% while EZ 2Y rose 1.1 bps to 2.5267% — a classic bear flattener in Europe, the opposite of the U.S. pattern. USD/EUR rate differentials now compress at the short end, which complicates dollar strength assumptions.

**BOJ hike and EM spillover:** The Bank of Japan hiked to its highest rate since 1995, tightening the global liquidity environment from a third direction simultaneously. With the yen at historic lows, the hike is partly a currency defense — but it also unwinds carry trades that fund EM positions. The EM stock and currency selloff, led by South Korea, reflects this carry unwind mechanism, not just idiosyncratic AI sentiment. Three major central banks tightening or holding hawkish in the same window withdraws global dollar-equivalent liquidity, which is the systemic read behind the equity and EM weakness.

**Hormuz re-opening offsets energy tail risk:** Qatar returned an empty LNG tanker through the Strait of Hormuz for the first time since the Iran war began, and a loaded tanker is now approaching the strait under the interim US-Iran peace deal. This is a partial release of the geopolitical energy premium — copper rallied on the growth optimism signal. Yet Lagarde simultaneously warned of prolonged energy shock effects, and the IEA flagged demand destruction from the war. The implication: energy markets are repricing a ceasefire premium, but the structural damage to demand is already embedded in the IEA forecast. Oil recovering to $93.11 on Israel-Lebanon strikes is a separate regional risk vector that partially offsets Hormuz relief.
