## Chain 1: Tech Selloff Meets Hawkish Rate Backdrop, Dollar Firms

**The driver:** A global rotation out of AI/semiconductor stocks — anchored by South Korea's Kospi falling 10% and SpaceX shares briefly collapsing before recovering — collided with a prior-session US yield curve that had already bear-steepened sharply, with the 10Y at 4.51% (+5 bps) and the 30Y at 4.95% (+5 bps), as bond traders reprice for a more hawkish Fed path.

**The chain:**

**Tech sentiment breaks, risk appetite narrows:** The Nasdaq Composite fell -2.21% to 25,587.04, dramatically underperforming the Dow Jones (-0.09%) and even the S&P 500 (-1.44%). This is a classic high-multiple compression event: when longer-duration yields rise, the present value of distant earnings falls hardest. The Nasdaq's multiple has been sustained by AI demand euphoria — the same narrative driving Nvidia toward a record corporate earnings print — and that consensus is now fragmenting. The Russell 2000's -0.96% decline confirms this isn't purely a megacap story; risk appetite is narrowing broadly.

**Yield curve context amplifies the pressure:** Going into today, the US curve had bear-steepened across the board, with the 6M at 3.98% (+6 bps), the 2Y at 4.24% (+5 bps), and the 10Y at 4.51% (+5 bps). The front end rising alongside the long end signals the market is not pricing imminent Fed cuts — it is pricing a scenario where inflation persists long enough to keep the Fed on hold or hiking. Bond traders are explicitly pointing to this week's personal spending data as a key recalibration trigger, which means the current repricing is provisional but directionally firm.

**Dollar strengthens, commodity currencies bear the brunt:** A hawkish Fed backdrop with risk-off equity flows is a straightforward dollar bid. DXY rose to 101.5740 (+0.16%), but the real pressure was in commodity-linked currencies: AUD/USD fell -1.33% to 0.6902, NZD/USD fell -1.11% to 0.5648, and USD/SEK surged +1.55% to 9.7683. These moves reflect two compounding forces — a stronger dollar and weakening global risk appetite — both of which punish high-beta, commodity-exposed FX. USD/MXN +1.30% and USD/ZAR +1.43% extend the same logic into EM.

**Tension — Eurozone and UK rates diverge sharply:** The prior session showed a stark split: Eurozone yields fell across the curve (EZ 10Y at 3.0094%, -2.7 bps; EZ 2Y at 2.5451%, -3.1 bps), while UK gilts surged (Gilt 10Y at 4.8984%, +9.8 bps; Gilt 20Y at 5.5808%, +9.9 bps). Lagarde explicitly said the ECB sees no need for a more forceful response and that inflation is returning to target — a dovish anchor that explains why Eurozone bonds rallied even as US and UK yields rose. The UK's gilt move is the outlier: BOE's Bailey called holding rates "a sensible decision," yet gilts sold off hard, suggesting the market is pricing UK fiscal or inflation risk independently of the BOE's guidance. EUR/GBP fell -0.19% to 0.8609, consistent with relative gilt weakness versus Bunds.

**Oil adds a disinflationary signal that cuts both ways:** Brent fell -1.71% to 75.76 and WTI fell -1.71% to 71.96, driven by the US easing Iran sanctions for 60 days, the Strait of Hormuz reopening, and Qatar signaling LNG normalization within weeks. Trump's pressure on oil firms compounds the bearish tone. Lower oil is disinflationary — in principle, it should relieve pressure on the hawkish end of the rates debate. But bond traders have not reacted that way: the yield curve continued to bear-steepen, suggesting the market views the Fed's concern as broader than just energy costs. Gold fell -0.90% to 4,092.60, with Deutsche Bank cutting forecasts by up to 22% — the precious metal is losing its inflation-hedge bid as the narrative shifts from supply-shock inflation to rate-driven financial tightening.

**BOJ signals more hikes, but USD/JPY barely moves — the key paradox:** BOJ's Ueda reiterated upside inflation risk and appropriate future rate hikes, yet USD/JPY sits at 161.6910, essentially flat (+0.07%). The BOJ has already hiked to its highest level since 1995, yet the yen refuses to strengthen materially. This reflects the carry trade's durability: with US 10Y at 4.51% versus JGB yields still far lower, the interest rate differential sustains yen selling. The PBOC adviser signaling a potential rate cut this year points in the opposite direction — easier Chinese monetary conditions would support risk assets in Asia (Hang Seng +0.42%, CSI 300 +0.03%) even as the rest of the world sells risk.

**What to watch:** First, this week's US personal spending and PCE data — if core PCE prints above consensus, the 2Y yield testing above 4.30% would confirm the hawkish repricing is fundamental, not positional; a miss would invite a sharp Nasdaq recovery. Second, watch USD/JPY at 162.00: if it breaks higher despite BOJ hawkishness, it signals the carry trade is overriding policy signals entirely and raises the probability of a BOJ intervention warning. Third, watch Gilt 10Y at 4.90%: if it breaks above that level without a corresponding UK data catalyst, the move starts to look like a fiscal credibility signal rather than a rates-repricing story, which would put BOE's "hold is sensible" framing under direct market pressure.
