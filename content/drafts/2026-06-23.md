**The driver:** The US Treasury's 60-day sanctions waiver on Iranian oil sales — confirmed by multiple outlets — is the single catalyst organizing today's cross-asset pattern, with Brent crude collapsing -4.83% to $84.36 and WTI falling -4.48% to $84.65 as Hormuz traffic begins recovering.

---

**The chain:**

**Iranian supply shock reversal → crude prices crater:** Treasury Secretary Bessent's 60-day sanctions waiver effectively unlocks Iranian barrels into the market, with Reuters reporting Asian refiners see little room to absorb them outside China — meaning the marginal buyer is already saturated. Brent at $84.36 (-4.83%) and WTI at $84.65 (-4.48%) reflect the market pricing a structural oversupply shift, not a one-day blip. The prior Hormuz disruption had been driving an energy-inflation premium across multiple asset classes; that premium is now unwinding simultaneously.

**Oil deflation → commodity currencies and petro-FX sold aggressively:** The oil crash hits currencies with direct energy-export exposure first. USD/NOK rises +1.00% to 9.7911 and USD/CAD rises +0.36% to 1.4211 — both petro-currencies weakening against the dollar. AUD/USD drops -1.23% to 0.6915 and NZD/USD falls -0.80% to 0.5668, commodity bloc FX broadly repricing lower as the inflation tailwind supporting these currencies reverses. USD/SEK surges +1.24% to 9.7382, consistent with a broad risk-off bid for the dollar outside safe-haven corridors.

**Peace-progress signal → copper diverges sharply from the oil move:** Copper surges +4.60% to 13483.7515 and aluminium rises +1.66% to 3654.0154 — a move that appears to contradict the energy selloff but is actually its complement. Bloomberg explicitly ties the copper rally to Iran peace-talk progress easing supply-chain and inflation fears, which restores confidence in global industrial demand. Where oil's move reflects a supply addition, copper's move reflects a demand-outlook upgrade: fewer sanctions → more trade flows → more construction and manufacturing activity in the pipeline.

**Conflicting forces hit gold — inflation hedge bid vs. rate-hike fear:** Gold falls -1.92% to 4111.4540, a move with two reinforcing mechanisms. First, lower oil reduces near-term inflation expectations, directly diminishing gold's store-of-value premium. Second — and critically — Deutsche Bank cut its gold forecasts by up to 22% as BofA warns the Fed is "losing patience" and will deliver a series of rate hikes, reversing earlier cuts. Higher real rates are the structural headwind: gold pays no yield, so rising rate expectations increase the opportunity cost of holding it. The two drivers point the same direction, making gold's decline the cleanest expression of the day's macro repricing.

**Gilt market breaks from the pack — BOE-specific tension:** UK gilts sell off sharply and in isolation: Gilt 5Y +9.8 bps to 4.3559%, Gilt 10Y +9.8 bps to 4.8984%, Gilt 20Y +9.9 bps to 5.5808%. This stands in direct contrast to US Treasuries (10Y -3.0 bps to 4.4600%) and EZ bonds (10Y -2.7 bps to 3.0094%), which both rallied as oil-driven inflation fears eased. Governor Bailey explicitly stated "holding is the right position" while acknowledging energy infrastructure damage and above-target energy prices — a signal the BOE sees stickier domestic inflation and is less willing to cut than peers. GBP/USD falls -0.37% to 1.3201 despite the yield backup, suggesting the gilt selloff reflects stagflation pricing rather than hawkish credibility.

**Fed repricing anchors the short end, steepens the curve:** SOFR at 3.6100% (-1.0 bps) and US 2Y at 4.1900% (-1.0 bps) dip marginally, but US 5Y falls -4.0 bps to 4.2300% and US 10Y falls -3.0 bps to 4.4600% — a bull steepening driven by the oil-deflation read-through to medium-term inflation. However, BofA's rate-hike warning and bond traders positioning for this week's PCE data mean the front end cannot rally cleanly. EUR/USD slips -0.42% to 1.1381 as Lagarde signals no need for a more forceful ECB response, cementing relative dollar strength in a world where the Fed is the hawkish outlier.
