**The chain:**

**Rate-hike repricing steepens the front end:** The 6M Treasury yield jumped +6.0 bps to 3.9800% and the 5Y rose +6.0 bps to 4.2900%, while the very front end (1M at 3.6600%, -3.0 bps) remains anchored near SOFR — producing a sharp bear steepening in the 6M–5Y segment. This is the classic signature of markets pricing in a sustained hiking cycle rather than a one-off adjustment: near-term policy rates are already set, but expectations for where rates will be over the next 1–5 years are being aggressively repriced higher. Bond traders, per Bloomberg, are explicitly looking to this week's personal spending data to validate the hawkish reprice.

**Equities take the brunt, with tech leading:** The Nasdaq 100 dropped -3.29% to 29347.2734 while the S&P 500 fell -1.44% to 7365.4600 and the Dow Jones lost only -0.09% to 51666.8398 — a classic duration-driven dispersion. Long-duration growth stocks (concentrated in the Nasdaq) are most sensitive to a rising discount rate, as their valuations rest on distant cash flows. The Dow's near-flat print reflects its tilt toward value and dividend names less exposed to duration risk. The VIX at 19.5800 (+13.31%) confirms this is not noise: options markets are pricing a sustained vol regime, consistent with the MarketWatch warning that the "tech fear gauge" is nearing a two-decade high.

**UK gilts diverge sharply from EZ peers — a transatlantic contagion signal:** Gilt 10Y rose +9.8 bps to 4.8984% and Gilt 20Y gained +9.9 bps to 5.5808%, dramatically outpacing EZ 10Y which actually fell -2.7 bps to 3.0094%. The mechanism: UK inflation dynamics more closely mirror the US supply-shock story (Canada's CPI just hit a 29-month high on energy), and BOE Governor Bailey explicitly flagged that energy infrastructure damage in the Middle East remains a live inflation risk even as he defended holding rates. EZ yields falling while UK and US yields rise reflects Lagarde's more sanguine inflation-to-target view versus the Anglo-American stagflation trap.

**Gold and silver crack under the rate-hike threat:** Gold fell -1.23% to 4130.3999 and silver collapsed -12.75% to 61.6800, with Deutsche Bank cutting gold forecasts by up to 22%. The mechanism is straightforward: precious metals carry no yield, so a credible hiking cycle raises the opportunity cost of holding them while simultaneously strengthening the dollar (DXY +0.37% to 101.3900). The Iran peace-talk progress (US Treasury authorizing Iranian oil sales under a 60-day license) also removes the geopolitical safe-haven bid that had been supporting gold — a double compression of both the rate and the fear premium simultaneously.

**Industrial metals join the sell-off despite copper's peace-talk tailwind:** Copper fell -3.59% to 6.1280, aluminium -3.81% to 4.3310, zinc -3.26% to 11.9400, and nickel -3.83% to 274.6545. This directly contradicts the Bloomberg headline that "Copper Rallies as Peace-Talk Progress Eases Inflation Fears" — the actual close tells a different story. The tension is real: Iran progress should reduce supply-chain stress and support industrial demand, but a Fed hiking cycle threatens to slow global growth and compress commodity demand. The growth-slowdown fear from higher US rates is currently winning over the geopolitical-relief trade.

**Oil softens as Hormuz reopens, gasoline diverges:** WTI fell -1.78% to 73.4900 and Brent -0.82% to 77.2600 as the Strait of Hormuz reopens and the US authorizes Iranian oil sales, unwinding the supply-shock premium. Gasoline (RBOB) dropped sharply at -4.15% to 2.8631, consistent with immediate supply normalization. The tension: Heating Oil gained +1.18% to 3.1297, suggesting traders see near-term supply relief in light products (summer driving) but retain a structural premium in distillates tied to Middle East infrastructure uncertainty — exactly the concern Bailey flagged. This split within energy is a micro-confirmation of the macro debate between transitory supply-shock relief and persistent inflation.
