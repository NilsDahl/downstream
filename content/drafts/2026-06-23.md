**The driver:** The BofA/Fed hawkish pivot — the Fed signaling a series of rate hikes to combat supply-shock inflation — is colliding with a geopolitical relief trade (US 60-day Iran sanctions waiver), producing a synchronized risk-off repricing across tech equities, gold, industrial metals, and oil simultaneously.

**The chain:**

**Hawkish Fed repricing steepens the US curve:** Going into today, the prior session saw the US yield curve bear-steepen materially — the 2Y rose +5.0 bps to 4.2400%, the 10Y rose +5.0 bps to 4.5100%, and the 30Y rose +5.0 bps to 4.9500% — while the front end (SOFR at 3.6100%, -1.0 bps; 1M at 3.6600%, -3.0 bps) barely moved, compressing near-term rate expectations while longer-end inflation premia widened. BofA's public call that the Fed "was willing to look through tariffs but is losing patience" after fresh supply shocks has forced bond traders to reprice terminal rate assumptions higher, making duration expensive and tightening financial conditions across risk assets.

**Tech equities take the hardest hit — valuation compression via rate transmission:** The Nasdaq 100 fell -3.29% to 29347.2734, dramatically underperforming the Dow Jones (-0.09% to 51666.8398) and even the S&P 500 (-1.44% to 7365.4600). This divergence is the textbook long-duration equity response: higher long-end yields mechanically compress discounted cash flow valuations for growth stocks whose earnings are weighted far into the future. The VIX surging +12.79% to 19.4900 confirms this is not orderly sector rotation but broad risk repricing, with the tech fear gauge cited in headlines as nearing a two-decade high adding a reflexive volatility feedback loop.

**Iran sanctions waiver crashes the geopolitical risk premium in oil:** The Treasury Department's 60-day Iran sanctions waiver, paired with Vice President Vance's "good foundation" language on a final deal, directly unlocks incremental Iranian supply into a market already reassessing demand. WTI fell -2.39% to 73.0300 and Brent fell -1.31% to 76.8800; RBOB gasoline fell -4.68% to 2.8472 — the sharpest single-asset move in energy — as the market front-runs lower refined-product prices that would otherwise sustain consumer inflation. This oil move partially undercuts the Fed's inflation-hawkishness rationale, creating a genuine cross-current: the central bank is tightening on supply-shock grounds precisely as the supply shock begins to ease.

**Gold and silver lose both their safe-haven and inflation-hedge bids simultaneously:** Gold fell -1.28% to 4128.5000, but silver's collapse of -12.92% to 61.5650 is the more violent signal. When the Fed turns hawkish, real yields rise, directly attacking gold's zero-coupon appeal; simultaneously, Iran peace-talk progress strips away the geopolitical risk premium that had been a second pillar of the gold bull case. Deutsche Bank cutting gold forecasts by up to 22% reinforces the capitulation dynamic. Silver's industrial component compounds its pain, as the next node explains.

The EUR also weakened -0.67% to 1.1386 despite Lagarde's relatively calm tone, suggesting the dollar tightening channel is dominating cross-asset FX.
