## Chain 1: Hawkish Fed Re-Pricing Collides With Geopolitical De-escalation

**The driver:** Bond traders repricing for a more hawkish Fed path — corroborated by a +5 bps bear steepening across the prior session's US curve to 4.51% on the 10Y and 4.95% on the 30Y — collides today with Strait of Hormuz reopening and US-Iran peace progress, producing a complex cross-asset session where rate fears dominate the equity and metals narrative while geopolitical relief shapes energy and copper.

---

**The chain:**

**US curve steepens, hawkish re-pricing sets the tone:** Going into today, the prior session saw the US yield curve shift materially higher at the belly and long end — 6M +6 bps to 3.98%, 2Y +5 bps to 4.24%, 10Y +5 bps to 4.51%, 30Y +5 bps to 4.95% — while SOFR eased 1 bps to 3.61%, preserving the short-end anchor. This bear steepening structure signals that markets are not pricing imminent cuts; they are pricing the possibility of additional hikes, or at minimum a materially longer hold. Bloomberg's report that bond traders burned by a failed Fed pivot call are now watching personal spending data for hawkish confirmation is the mechanism: forced repositioning out of duration is the trade, not a fundamental growth deterioration.

**Equities reprice rate sensitivity, not recession:** The S&P 500 fell -1.44% to 7365.46 and the Nasdaq Composite fell -2.21% to 25587.04, while the Dow Jones declined only -0.09% to 51666.84. This dispersion is the critical read — the Nasdaq's outsized underperformance relative to the Dow reflects duration compression hitting long-multiple growth names hardest, not a broad cyclical growth scare. The Russell 2000 fell -0.96% to 2975.48, consistent with rate sensitivity rather than a flight from risk uniformly. The VIX surged +12.79% to 19.49, and headlines flag a tech fear gauge nearing a two-decade high — elevated implied vol reinforces that options markets are pricing the rate uncertainty premium, not a single shock event.

**Gold and silver break down as rate-hike fears override safe-haven demand:** Gold fell -1.26% to 4129.00 and silver collapsed -5.95% to 61.63. The mechanism is straightforward: precious metals are non-yielding assets whose opportunity cost rises directly with rate expectations. Deutsche Bank cutting gold forecasts by up to 22% is not just a price call — it is a signal that sell-side conviction in the Fed pivot trade, which drove gold's prior rally, is being explicitly withdrawn. The magnitude of silver's move relative to gold (nearly 5x the percentage decline) reflects silver's higher beta to industrial growth expectations as well as its monetary component — both headwinds are hitting simultaneously. Platinum fell -1.04% to 1652.90 and palladium -1.48% to 1238.50, confirming the move is across the precious metals complex, not idiosyncratic to gold.

**Geopolitical de-escalation relieves the energy risk premium — but demand takes over:** Brent crude fell -1.31% to 76.88 and WTI fell -2.37% to 73.05, with RBOB gasoline down -4.80% to 2.8435. The Strait of Hormuz reopening removes the acute supply disruption fear that had inflated the geopolitical risk premium; tanker traffic normalizing means the physical supply shock is unwinding. Natural gas fell -1.78% to 3.1950 on cooler weather forecasts and reduced LNG flows, compounding the energy complex's weakness. Lagarde explicitly stating the ECB sees no need for a more forceful response because inflation is returning to target is consistent with a world where the energy impulse is fading — reduced Hormuz disruption is disinflationary at the margin, which creates a direct tension with the hawkish Fed re-pricing driving the bond and equity moves.

**Industrial metals sell off sharply — the copper peace-talk narrative contradicts the price:** Copper fell -3.64% to 6.1250, aluminium -3.81% to 4.3310, zinc -3.26% to 11.9400, and nickel -3.83% to 274.6545. Bloomberg reported copper rallied on US-Iran peace talk progress easing inflation fears — but the snapshot shows copper significantly lower, not higher. The Bloomberg headline and the price action are in direct contradiction. The most coherent resolution: any intraday copper pop on peace-talk optimism was overwhelmed by the broader industrial metals de-rating driven by rate-hike fears compressing global growth expectations and by the stronger DXY (+0.34% to 101.365), which mechanically pressures dollar-denominated commodity prices. This tension — geopolitical relief constructive for copper demand, but Fed hawkishness and USD strength destructive for pricing — resolved decisively in favor of the macro rate/dollar forces.

**FX: EUR leads the dollar move, UK gilts signal idiosyncratic stress:** EUR/USD fell -0.66% to 1.1387 as the DXY firmed, consistent with hawkish Fed re-pricing widening the US-EZ rate differential — going into today, EZ yields were actually falling (EZ 10Y -2.7 bps to 3.0094%, EZ 2Y -3.1 bps) against rising US yields, directly widening the spread and mechanically cheapening the euro. The critical divergence is UK gilts: Gilt 5Y rose +9.8 bps to 4.3559%, Gilt 10Y +9.8 bps to 4.8984%, and Gilt 20Y +9.9 bps to 5.5808% — a uniform, steep rise that is notably more aggressive than moves in either US Treasuries or EZ bonds in the prior session. BOE Governor Bailey calling "holding" the sensible decision even as energy prices remain above pre-war levels suggests the BOE is not behind the gilt selloff — it may reflect UK-specific fiscal risk or inflation persistence premium building independently. GBP/USD held near flat at -0.02% to 1.3205, meaning the gilt selloff is not a sterling event, which points toward a domestic inflation or supply concern rather than a currency crisis dynamic.
